From idea to prototype

What problem & for whom

For whom: People who open the fridge, see perfectly good vegetables, and still end up with olive oil and salt — because creativity in the kitchen doesn't come naturally to everyone.

Problem: "I have lettuce, tomato, avocado — vinaigrette? Yogurt?" They want a quick answer and a real recipe. No guesswork, no sign-up, no friction.

This: Pick ingredients → get a matching dressing + recipe + one kitchen trick. One click. Done.

Design decisions

The game that couldn't be: I wanted ingredients to appear visually on a plate as you picked them — a little game where your salad builds itself in real time. The AI only rendered colored dots (red for tomato, green for cucumber). The plate didn't make it. The game moved, not died — the playful layer shifted to the ingredient animation instead, a small choreography that makes picking feel like playing.

From list to interface: The original layout was a vertical stack of boxes. I moved to a side-by-side layout on desktop so both panels are visible at once. On mobile, the output box peeks from below — a deliberate cue to reduce friction and make the next step obvious without any instructions.

Zero instructions: Instead of onboarding copy, I added an animation that highlights the ingredients one by one. The interface teaches itself. First interaction in under 3 seconds, with zero reading required.

Minimum viable effort: One click = one full salad with dressing and recipe. I kept asking myself "can I remove one more step?" until the answer was no.
